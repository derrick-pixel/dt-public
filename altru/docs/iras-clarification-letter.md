**From:** Derrick Teo, Founder, Altru Asia Pte Ltd · derrick@elitez.asia
**To:** Inland Revenue Authority of Singapore — Charities Branch
**Date:** 11 May 2026
**Re:** Tax-deduction receipts under s.37(3)(b) Income Tax Act for gifts routed through a wedding-giving platform

---

## 1 · What we are asking

We respectfully request **written guidance** on the correct posture for the issuance of tax-deduction receipts under s.37(3)(b) of the Income Tax Act ("ITA") for gifts that reach an IPC-registered charity via Altru's wedding-giving platform.

The specific posture we propose is:
- The **wedding couple** is the named donor of record on the IRAS receipt.
- The **IPC charity** issues the receipt in the couple's name, supported by transaction metadata supplied by Altru.
- The receipt is issued in the partner charity's ordinary cycle (no role for Altru in the actual issuance).

We would like IRAS confirmation that this posture is acceptable, or alternatively guidance on the correct posture.

---

## 2 · About Altru and the fund flow

Altru Asia Pte Ltd is a newly incorporated Singapore Pte Ltd, regulated under the Charities Act 1994 as a Commercial Fundraiser. We operate altru.asia, a platform that enables wedding guests to direct a portion of their ang-bao gift to one of three curated IPC partner charities of the couple's choice.

The fund flow (annotated):
1. The wedding guest initiates a gift on the platform and pays via PayNow into Altru's escrow arrangement (via our licensed PSP, HitPay).
2. A 14-day couple-authorisation window begins. During the window, the couple reviews the gift and authorises (or does not authorise) release.
3. On authorisation, the **charity portion** of the gift is remitted in full ("paid in gross", per §B.1 of the Code for Commercial Fundraisers) to the partner charity's PayNow-UEN. The personal portion is remitted to the couple's nominated account.
4. If the couple does not authorise within 14 days, the gift is auto-refunded to the guest. There is no possibility of the gift being released to the charity without explicit couple authorisation.
5. Altru separately invoices the partner charity for a 5% platform fee. This is not deducted from the donation.

The platform's "couple-as-named-donor" structure is described publicly at <https://altru.asia/charities/escrow.html> and the privacy treatment is described at <https://altru.asia/privacy.html>.

---

## 3 · Why we propose the couple as named donor

We propose the couple as the named donor because:

- **The couple exercises the relevant donative intent.** The selection of the charity, the split between personal and charitable portions, and the *authorisation* of release are all decisions made by the couple. The wedding guest sends a gift to the couple; the couple decides that a portion of it should reach the charity.
- **The couple bears the actual decision-making risk.** Until couple authorisation, the funds are at risk of auto-refund. The act that fixes the gift to the charity is the couple's authorisation, not the guest's payment.
- **The platform mirrors traditional ang-bao behaviour.** In a physical ang-bao context, the guest gives to the couple, and the couple subsequently donates to the charity. Altru digitises this exact sequence; the couple is the donor at the moment the charity acquires the funds.
- **Practical clarity.** A wedding routinely involves many guests, often with incomplete contact information. Issuing receipts to each guest creates significant administrative overhead for both Altru and the charity, with little obvious tax-policy gain.

We acknowledge that the wedding guest is the *original source* of funds, and we will disclose this on the platform and in the metadata passed to the charity. Our reading of s.37(3)(b) and the Charities Act framework is that the IPC's receipt should follow the donative intent and the moment of charitable irrevocability, both of which sit with the couple.

## 4 · Specific questions to IRAS

1. **Primary question.** In the fund flow described in Section 2, is the couple (one or both partners, by election) the appropriate named donor on the s.37(3)(b) tax-deduction receipt issued by the IPC partner charity?

2. **NRIC capture.** Altru proposes to capture the NRIC of the named donor (one or both partners) at couple onboarding, and to pass this NRIC together with the gift amount to the partner charity for the receipt. Is this acceptable for s.37(3)(b) purposes?

3. **Co-donor election.** Where both partners wish to be named donors, may the gift be split between them in any proportion they elect at onboarding (e.g., 50/50, or 100/0)?

4. **Receipt timing.** The charity will issue the receipt in its ordinary cycle, supported by Altru's monthly remittance metadata. Is there a maximum gap permitted between the date of release of funds and the date of receipt issuance for the purposes of donor tax-deduction claim?

5. **Refund treatment.** If a gift is auto-refunded (Section 2 step 4), no charitable transfer has occurred and no receipt is issued. We assume this is uncontroversial; please confirm.

6. **Anonymous-donor option.** Some wedding guests may elect not to share NRIC. In that case, no receipt is requested and no tax deduction can be claimed by anyone. We assume this is uncontroversial; please confirm.

7. **Records.** Altru maintains immutable audit logs of every state transition with 5-year retention under the Charities Act. Are there any IRAS-specific record-retention or evidentiary requirements we should layer on top, beyond those already maintained by the partner charity for its receipts?
