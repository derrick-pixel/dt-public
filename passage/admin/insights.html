Section 01

Industry Overview — Why Casket Pricing Stays Hidden

Singapore's funeral economy bundles caskets inside opaque all-in packages because the buying decision happens within hours of bereavement, when comparison shopping is structurally impossible. This is the gap Passage exists to close.

Across Singapore's ~24,000 annual deaths, the casket purchase is almost never a standalone consumer decision. Funeral directors quote a single "all-in" figure between S$8,000 and S$15,000 covering wake space, embalming, hearse, rites, refreshments, and casket. Within that bundle, the casket alone is marked up to between S$2,000 and S$4,000 — verbatim from Passage's own anchoring testimonial: "We were quoted S$3,800 as part of the 'standard package'. We only found out afterwards that the casket alone was marked up from a S$600 cost price."

The hiding works because the MOH death-certificate flow compresses decision-making: the certificate is issued within hours, Mandai cremation slots are booked under finite daily capacity, and bereaved families have no time to research alternatives. Directors know this. Pressure tactics are common — some threaten to withhold services if families decline their in-house casket. Casket-only pricing is deliberately not published, even on category-leading parlour websites.

Three structural shifts are now eroding the bundle's defensibility:

Shift 01
Direct cremation rising
An estimated ~35% of Singapore families now choose minimal-wake or direct-cremation routes (industry estimate). The traditional bundle's price-anchoring weakens once the wake itself is optional.
Shift 02
Pre-arrangement normalising
Living seniors increasingly plan ahead, removing the time-pressure that historically protected the bundled-pricing model. Pre-buyers comparison-shop with calm.
Shift 03
Consumer-rights media coverage
Straits Times and CNA cost-of-dying coverage (2023–2025) has put casket markups on the public record. Anti-pressure consumer sentiment is now organised, vocal, and shopping for transparent alternatives.

Sources: SITE-INTEL.md (verbatim Passage page content). Volumetric figures (24K deaths, 65/35 split) flagged as industry estimate, anchored to Straits Times / CNA cost-of-dying coverage 2023–2025 and MOH death-certificate records.

Section 05

Competitive Gaps & Passage Differentiation

Five deck-grounded advantages traditional parlour bundles structurally cannot copy without dismantling their own margin model.

  1. All-in transparent published pricing
    Passage publishes S$800 / S$1,000 / S$1,200 all-in including delivery, venue positioning, engraved nameplate, and GST. Parlour competitors hide casket cost between S$2,000–4,000 inside an S$8–15K bundle. The gap is not a price war — it is a disclosure regime mismatch the incumbents cannot close without re-pricing their wake business.
  2. 3-hour island-wide delivery with surge options
    Standard 3-hour delivery to any address in Singapore. Surge options: +S$75 for 2-hour, +S$150 for 1-hour emergency. No parlour publishes equivalent SLAs because their casket is bundled in-house and tied to a wake-venue booking, not a logistics product.
  3. FSC-certified timber + local Singapore assembly
    FSC Chain-of-Custody certified, 60% locally-sourced FSC timber, 4.2 t CO2e per casket total lifecycle, <5% material waste at assembly, 100% local workforce, Progressive Wage Model compliant. The parlour ecosystem imports tropical hardwood through wholesale chains with no published carbon accounting.
  4. 30% net profit to charity — binding, audited, quarterly published
    Charity allocation is locked into the company constitution, not a marketing line. FY2024 donations: S$48,200 across Singapore Hospice Council, SASCO Senior Citizens' Home, and Metta Welfare Association. Audited and quarterly published. No incumbent operates an equivalent binding mechanism.
  5. Free casket programme — proven, not aspirational
    FY2024: 47 free caskets given — 31 social-worker referrals + 16 direct family applications, channelled via 8 welfare-org partners. Eligibility: ComCare recipients, deceased without next-of-kin, social-worker referrals, compassionate-basis assessments. Parlour packages have no equivalent at-cost commitment.

Next-Best Alternatives — Six Substitutes

When a Singapore family does not buy from Passage, where does the casket-spend go instead? Six alternatives, each with a threat level reflecting how much demand they pull from Passage's transparent-DTC wedge.

Traditional parlour package
High
What it is
Singapore Casket, Ang Chin Moh, Trinity, Nam Shan — full-bundle wake + casket + rites at S$8–15K.
Pulls from
Cost-conscious bereaved (under time pressure) and pre-arrangement seniors with traditional family expectations.
Why it wins
One-stop convenience under bereavement-grade time compression. Cultural defaults.
Why it loses
Hidden markup of S$2–4K on casket alone. No published itemisation. Pressure tactics increasingly resented.
Eco / paper DTC casket
Medium
What it is
The Eco Casket (Selangor) — Finoboard biodegradable casket, imported into SG.
Pulls from
Eco / values-driven buyers prioritising biodegradability over aesthetic.
Why it wins
Genuinely lower-impact materials. Strong sustainability story.
Why it loses
Cross-border logistics, no SG assembly, no Tripartite/PWM workforce stack, no charity binding mechanism.
Direct cremation (no-wake)
Medium
What it is
Stripped-back service: pickup, parlour-supplied basic casket, Mandai cremation slot. Total S$1.8–3.2K.
Pulls from
Cost-conscious bereaved and anti-pressure consumers willing to forgo wake.
Why it wins
Lowest total spend. Gaining cultural acceptance — ~35% of SG families now no-wake.
Why it loses
Casket line still bundled and marked-up internally; Passage Serene + Mandai slot beats it on transparency and total cost.
DIY / no-casket-purchase
Low
What it is
Family arranges directly with Mandai using a parlour-loaned or simple wood casket; no DTC purchase.
Pulls from
Hardship cases not yet routed through social-worker referral. Edge cases.
Why it wins
Near-zero casket spend.
Why it loses
Logistical burden on grieving family. Many such cases qualify for Passage's free-casket programme — a structurally better outcome.
Hospital basic casket
Medium
What it is
Hospital-routed minimum-viable casket option, often via in-house mortuary partner.
Pulls from
Cost-conscious bereaved at point of death-certification. Captive-channel buyers.
Why it wins
Zero search cost. Hospital is the trusted authority in the moment.
Why it loses
Limited choice, no transparency, no FSC story, no charity allocation. Passage's 24/7 helpline + 3-hour delivery defuses the captive-channel advantage.
Columbarium-only path
Low
What it is
Family pre-buys columbarium niche; casket purchase deferred to any minimum-viable provider at point of death.
Pulls from
Pre-arrangement seniors who pre-bought niche but never selected a casket provider.
Why it wins
Niche secured early; defers casket decision indefinitely.
Why it loses
Defers, does not solve the casket transparency problem. Passage's pre-arrangement option locks in price ahead of family bereavement.

Threat levels reflect estimated demand-pull on Passage's transparent-DTC wedge, not absolute market size. Provider names verbatim from SITE-INTEL.md section 8 (real direct competitors).
